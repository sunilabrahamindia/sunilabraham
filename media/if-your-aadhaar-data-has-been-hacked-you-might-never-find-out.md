**If Your Aadhaar Data Has Been Hacked, You Might Never Find Out** is a news report published by *The Quint* (originally published on *BloombergQuint*) on 3 March 2017, written by Mayank Jain. The article examines the absence of breach notification provisions in the Aadhaar Act following an alleged biometric data breach involving Axis Bank, Suvidhaa Infoserve and eMudhra. It features commentary from Sunil Abraham, executive director of Centre for Internet and Society, alongside legal experts Apar Gupta and Rahul Matthan, analysing statutory gaps in data protection, regulatory oversight and grievance redressal mechanisms under the Aadhaar framework.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>The debate over the security of data collected and stored under the Aadhaar project is heating up. While the Unique Identification Project has always had strong supporters and equally strong detractors, the latest controversy has been sparked off by an alleged breach of biometric data.</p>

<p>On 25 February, <em>Mint</em> reported that the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI) had detected a breach of biometric data and filed a police complaint on 15 February against Axis Bank Ltd, business correspondent Suvidhaa Infoserve and e-sign provider eMudhra with the allegation of impersonation using illegally stored biometric information.</p>

<p>These entities deny storage of any data and claim that the business correspondent was merely testing the platform which accidentally sent authentication requests to the live server instead of the testing one.</p>

<p>Still, the incident has sparked concerns about the security of data in the possession with the UIDAI and also the redressal mechanism in the case of a breach like this.</p>

<blockquote>Interestingly, it turns out that the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act is silent on making the UIDAI liable for reporting any breaches.</blockquote>

<p>Legal experts that <em>BloombergQuint</em> spoke to point out that while the international principles of data collection require that any breach or abnormal activity be revealed to the users and authorities quickly, the Aadhaar regulations give no such assurance.</p>

<p>While banks are not required to disclose details of the breach to the public, they are required to report it to regulators immediately. Customer protection provisions also ensure deposit protection to some extent.</p>

<p>Delhi-based lawyer Apar Gupta said that a user may never really get to know if there was a data breach in the Aadhaar database which compromised their personal data, unless the authority chooses to voluntarily disclose it.</p>

<p>Adding that the authority is "whole and sole" and it isn't liable to even disclose it under the Right To Information Act or to the parliament, Gupta said:</p>

<blockquote>There is no provision under the act for notification to the public that there's been a breach of their data. The breach includes both hacking of identity data and unauthorised authentication carried out by someone impersonating a citizen.</blockquote>

<p>A broad comparison can be drawn to system breach in the banking system last year which led to 32 lakh debit cards being compromised.</p>

<p>In contrast, there is no regulatory oversight of the UIDAI which keeps it away from scrutiny, Gupta said. "There is an absence of a breach notification requirement under the Act," he added.</p>

<p>Rahul Matthan, partner at law firm Trilegal said that all technological systems are likely to be breached at one point or the other and the Aadhaar Act has strong provisions against nefarious elements wronging the system.</p>

<p>"There is a clear violation of the law in this case no matter who has done it," Matthan said while adding that it is not clear where the violation has happened.</p>

<blockquote>If a bank employee steals your money, there is nothing you can do since the bank expects all employees to behave properly. It's the same thing here as all participants in the process are expected to keep information safe.<br/>
<em>Rahul Matthan, Partner, Trilegal</em></blockquote>

<p>Matthan said that storing of biometrics is wrong and illegal and the concern stems from the potentiality of people using this information left, right and centre.</p>

<p>He also added that protective provisions of the IT Act will apply to Aadhaar as well.</p>

<blockquote>"Any data breach is required to be disclosed under the IT Act but whether that is followed or not is a separate question," he said. Every authentication under the system is reported to the citizens through an SMS which is effectively informing them of unusual activity, he added.</blockquote>

<p>However, there remains a loophole here for those who do not have their correct phone numbers updated in the Aadhaar system.</p>

<p>Gupta also said that even if one gets to know about their Aadhaar being misused, there is no guarantee under the Act that a redressal will be provided.</p>

<p>In chapter 7 of the Aadhaar regulations, there is a provision for a grievance redressal mechanism whereby a person can approach a call centre through phone or email which will provide residents with a tracking number till the matter is closed.</p>

<p>Gupta believes that this is not enough, saying that:</p>

<blockquote>Even the IT Act has the same absence of reporting principles as the Aadhaar Act so it is substantively insufficient. There is no requirement for the call centre to give you a reasoned order like a public authority does. There is no specification to give you a reason if they don't agree with you and why they can't help you. The process doesn't ensure that people's dispute will be determined by the principles of natural justice.</blockquote>

<p>Additionally, Sunil Abraham, executive director of Bangalore-based Centre for Internet and Society told <em>BloombergQuint</em> that a privacy law was promised when the Aadhaar Act was being passed in the parliament. That never happened so the system remains deficient on redressal.</p>

<blockquote>I don't think there is a data breach provision in the act. They said don't worry, a privacy bill is coming which never happened. But whether we have privacy bill or not, as long as there is centralised biometric data, we are in constant danger.<br/>
<em>Sunil Abraham, Executive Director, Centre for Internet and Society</em></blockquote>

## Context and Background

This article emerged during a critical phase when the Aadhaar project faced mounting scrutiny over data security whilst rapidly expanding its usage mandate across banking, welfare delivery and telecommunications sectors. The incident that sparked the report involved a police complaint filed by UIDAI on 15 February 2017 against Axis Bank, Suvidhaa Infoserve and eMudhra for allegedly storing biometric data illegally and using it for impersonation.

The controversy highlighted a fundamental gap in the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act 2016, which had been passed by Parliament just eight months earlier as a money bill, avoiding scrutiny by the Rajya Sabha. Unlike banking regulations that mandated reporting breaches to regulators, the Aadhaar Act imposed no obligation on UIDAI to notify affected individuals or any oversight body when breaches occurred. This created an accountability vacuum, as UIDAI operated without regulatory oversight and was exempt from Right to Information Act disclosure requirements.

The comparison drawn to the 2016 debit card breach that compromised 3.2 million cards was instructive. Banks, despite lacking public disclosure requirements, faced regulatory consequences and had to compensate affected customers under established banking protections. By contrast, Aadhaar's statutory framework provided no comparable safeguards or remedies for citizens whose biometric data might be compromised.

The grievance redressal mechanism outlined in Chapter 7 of the Aadhaar regulations amounted to little more than a call centre helpline that issued tracking numbers. As Apar Gupta noted, it lacked the procedural rigour of administrative tribunals, with no requirements for reasoned orders, timelines for resolution or adherence to principles of natural justice. This was particularly problematic given biometrics cannot be reset like passwords or PIN numbers.

The article appeared amid parliamentary and legal debates over the constitutional validity of Aadhaar's passage as a money bill. Privacy concerns were escalating, leading to the landmark challenge before the Supreme Court that would ultimately result in the August 2017 Puttaswamy judgment recognising privacy as a fundamental right. The promise of a comprehensive privacy law that Sunil Abraham referenced remained unfulfilled for several more years until the Digital Personal Data Protection Act debates began in 2022. The fundamental tension the article identified between centralised biometric databases and privacy protection continues to shape policy debates around digital identity systems.
