**Modi's Valley Hug Sparks Swadeshi Talk** is a news report published by *The Times of India* on 6 October 2015, written by Shilpa Phadnis and Sujit John. The article documents domestic criticism of Prime Minister Narendra Modi's Silicon Valley visit, particularly concerns that the government was outsourcing critical Digital India infrastructure to global corporations rather than building domestic capabilities. It features commentary from Sunil Abraham warning that software patent litigation could stifle Indian innovation, citing the Ericsson-Micromax patent dispute as evidence of how patent enforcement disproportionately burdens local manufacturers.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>BENGALURU: His warm hug of Facebook's Mark Zuckerberg will perhaps be the most abiding image of Narendra Modi's visit to the Silicon Valley. But that embrace, and what it conveyed, is now becoming the subject for an intense debate among techies here. Is Modi giving in too much to the Googles and Facebooks of the world, when there is so much technology talent within India? Is he taking the easy way out by handing out critical pieces of his Digital India vision to global incumbents rather than build domestic capabilities?</p>

<p>"When the government wanted to build a citizen engagement platform earlier this year, they depended on Google. Now when they want Wi-Fi in railway stations, it's again Google. These are things that can be done by our companies, otherwise we will not be able to create our own digital industry. Remember, we are the people who built Aadhaar," said an industry veteran who did not want to be named.</p>

<p>Nitin Pai, co-founder of Takshashila, an independent policy research and advocacy body that provides services for government agencies, NGOs and corporations, said Modi's team should make a careful distinction between national interest and MNCs' commercial interest. "Many MNCs have come forward to participate in Digital India initiatives. The government will have to look at offering sufficient incentives for innovation to domestic tech companies, many of whom are coming with innovative business models," he said.</p>

<p>Speaking to a cross-section of tech companies here, two sets of concerns emerge. One relates to the concessions that will work in favour of global incumbents and against newcomers the latter is likely to add more value in the long term. This includes the issue of net neutrality, on which the government is seen to be waffling, and the guidelines issued by the Indian Patent Office declaring that software is patentable in India.</p>

<p>The new guidelines will make it easier for companies to file for software patents in India. But software patenting has become hugely controversial globally, because innovations in the area are often just incremental, and come on top of other software programs. Besides, patenting is expensive and is often the subject of litigation, both of which work against small ventures with little resources. Companies like Google have spent billions of dollars to buy patents.</p>

<p>Venkatesh Hariharan, member of software product think-tank iSpirt, said the new guidelines would make it easier for bigger companies to file software patents, but for smaller firms and startups, the move could be detrimental. "They could end up fighting patent litigations. In the US, 37% of patent litigation is around software and business patents," he said.</p>

<p>Sunil Abraham, executive director in research organization Centre for Internet and Society, fears litigation could kill local innovation. He cited a recent example where a Delhi high court order asked Indian handset manufacturer Micromax to pay 1.25%‚Äì2% of the selling price of its devices to Ericsson that had claimed infringement of patents.</p>

<p>The other set of issues relates to certain rules and regulations in India that place significant obstacles before small technology ventures. This is resulting in many ventures shifting their registered offices to Singapore or the US.</p>

<p>Albinder Dindsa's on-demand delivery service Grofers is among the latest to create a holding company in Singapore. "It is challenging to do business in India. Even opening a bank account took time. We thought it would be easier to do an IPO if we are in Singapore," he said.</p>

<p>A senior industry analyst who did not want to be named said that till last year, two out of four ventures were moving out of India, but now that figure is three out of four. "I would expect the government to do something about it. In fact, the finance minister did say in June that the issues would be addressed within 30 days. But nothing has happened."</p>

<p>Also, it is expensive for angel investors overseas to invest in Indian startups.</p>

<p>India disallows startups from offering stock options to foreign nationals, which makes it difficult for them to access seasoned mentors.</p>

## Context and Background

Prime Minister Modi's Silicon Valley visit from 26-27 September 2015 marked the first visit by an Indian leader to the West Coast in over three decades. The centrepiece was a Facebook townhall with Mark Zuckerberg watched by millions globally, during which Modi discussed Digital India initiatives and his personal story. The visit also included stops at Google headquarters, where the company announced free Wi-Fi at 400 Indian railway stations, and meetings with Tesla and other technology companies.

The domestic reaction among India's technology community was mixed. While some celebrated the international attention and investment commitments, others questioned whether the government was effectively outsourcing critical digital infrastructure to foreign corporations when India possessed substantial indigenous technical capacity. The reference to Aadhaar highlighted that India had successfully built one of the world's largest biometric identity systems using domestic talent, raising questions about why similar capacity-building was not being prioritised for Digital India.

The software patent guidelines referenced in the article were issued by the Indian Patent Office in August 2015, revising earlier 2013 guidelines. The new framework liberalised patentability criteria for computer-related inventions, particularly allowing patents where software provided "technical advancement" even on known hardware. This represented a significant departure from India's historically restrictive approach to software patents, which had aligned with jurisdictions like the European Union in treating standalone software as non-patentable abstract ideas.

The Ericsson-Micromax case that Sunil Abraham cited involved standard-essential patents for telecommunications technologies. The Delhi High Court's 2015 interim order requiring Micromax to pay royalties to Ericsson exemplified how patent enforcement could disadvantage domestic hardware manufacturers. For small Indian technology companies, the combination of expensive patent filing, uncertain examination standards, and the risk of infringement litigation from foreign patent holders created significant barriers to innovation and market entry.

The broader issue of startups incorporating overseas reflected structural obstacles in India's regulatory environment. Factors included cumbersome incorporation procedures, capital controls restricting foreign investment flows, tax complexities, and regulations preventing issuance of employee stock options to foreign nationals, which limited access to international mentorship and expertise. By 2015, an estimated 75% of Indian startups were creating holding companies in Singapore or Delaware to access global capital markets and simplify cross-border operations.
