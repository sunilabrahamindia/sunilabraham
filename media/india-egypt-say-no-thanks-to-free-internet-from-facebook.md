**India, Egypt Say No Thanks to Free Internet from Facebook** is a report published by *The Washington Post* on 28 January 2016, written by Annie Gowen. The article examines the growing opposition to Facebook's Free Basics programme in India and its ban in Egypt, documenting how critics argued that zero-rated walled gardens violated net neutrality principles and disadvantaged the poor. It includes commentary from Sunil Abraham, who described India's regulatory proceedings as a crucial test case that would influence net neutrality frameworks globally.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p><strong>ALWAR, India</strong> ‚Äî Connecting people to the Internet is not easy in this impoverished farming district of wheat and millet fields, where working camels can be glimpsed along roads that curve through the low-slung Aravalli Hills.</p>

<p>So when Facebook chief executive Mark Zuckerberg helicoptered in about a year ago to visit a small computer lab and tout Internet for all, Osama Manzar, director of India's Digital Empowerment Foundation, was thrilled.</p>

<p>But when Manzar tried Facebook's limited free Internet service, he was bitterly disappointed. The app, called Free Basics, is a pared-down version of Facebook with other services such as weather reports and job listings.</p>

<p>"I feel betrayed ‚Äî not only betrayed but upset and angry," Manzar said. "He said we're going to solve the problem with access and bandwidth. But Facebook is not the Internet."</p>

<p>Zuckerberg launched his sweeping Internet.org initiative in 2013 as a way to provide 4 billion people in the developing world with Web access, which he says he sees as a basic human right.</p>

<p>But the initiative has hit a major snag in India, where in recent months Free Basics has been embroiled in controversy ‚Äî with critics saying that the app, which provides limited access to the Web, does a disservice to the poor and violates the principles of "net neutrality," which holds that equal access to the Internet should be unfettered to all.</p>

<p>Activist groups such as Save the Internet, professors from leading universities and tech titans such as Nandan Nilekani, the co-founder of Infosys, have spoken out against it. Another well-known Indian entrepreneur dubbed it "poor Internet for poor people."</p>

<p>The debate escalated in recent weeks after India's telecommunications regulator suspended Free Basics as it weighs whether such plans are fair, with new rules expected by the end of the month.</p>

<p>A week later, Free Basics was banned in Egypt with little explanation, prompting concern that the backlash could spread to other markets. More recently, Google pulled out of the app in Zambia after a trial period. An estimated 15 million people are using Free Basics in 37 countries, including 1 million in India.</p>

<p>"It's a very important test case for what will be India's network neutrality regime," said Sunil Abraham of the Center for Internet and Society in Bangalore.</p>

<p>India's debate could affect the way other countries address the question of whether it is fair for Internet service providers to price websites differently. The U.S. Federal Communications Commission's rules on net neutrality went into effect only in June.</p>

<p>Officials at Facebook launched an advertising blitz to counteract the negative publicity. "Who could possibly be against this?" Zuckerberg wondered in a Times of India editorial on Dec. 28.</p>

<p>"I think we've been a bit surprised by the strong reaction," said Chris Daniels, Facebook's vice president for Internet.org. "Fundamentally, the reason for the surprise is that the program is doing good. It's bringing people online who are moving onto the broader Internet."</p>

<p>India, a country of 1.2 billion, has the second-highest number of Internet users in the world, but an estimated 80 percent of the population does not have Internet access.</p>

<p>India's tech-savvy prime minister, Narendra Modi, is trying to combat this with an ambitious "Digital India" plan to link 250,000 village centers with fiber-optic cable and extend mobile coverage. He has turned to the Indian tech community as well as Silicon Valley for help, securing an agreement with Google to provide free WiFi in railway stations.</p>

<p>India has 130 million Facebook users, second only to the United States, and is a key market as the social-media giant looks to expand beyond the developed world, where its growth has slowed.</p>

<p>"If Facebook manages to get another half a billion users in India, that's a valuable set of eyeballs to sell to a political party or corporation," Abraham said.</p>

<p>Facebook has long said that its program is about altruism, not eyeballs.</p>

<p>But it does reap new customers. Those who buy a SIM card from Facebook's local mobile partner, Reliance Communications, are then prompted to pay for additional data. About 40 percent who sign up for Free Basics buy a data plan to move to the wider Web after 30 days, Daniels said.</p>

<p>The service is still running despite the India suspension. A Reliance spokesman said it is in "testing mode" and is not being promoted.</p>

<p>"The thing people forget about Free Basics is that it's intended to be a temporary transition for people to give them a taste of the Internet and sign up. It's a marketing program for the carrier in some sense," said David Kirkpatrick, author of "The Facebook Effect." But he added: "The idea that it's some kind of alternative Internet that's a discriminatory gesture to the poor is the prevailing view among the Indian intelligentsia. It's fundamentally misunderstood."</p>

<p>Facebook has pledged to open up to new scrutiny the selection process for companies with new applications, Daniels said. That is a response to concerns by many in India's tech community that Facebook's process put India's fledgling start-ups at a disadvantage.</p>

<p>The project's proponents say that India's needs are so great it cannot afford to suspend one program that could help.</p>

<p>Mahesh Uppal, a telecommunications consultant, notes that more than 10 percent of the country does not have mobile phone coverage and that India's progress in extending fiber-optic cable to village centers is proceeding at a glacial pace. Modi had set a goal of linking all 250,000 by 2016, but only 27,000 have cable so far and it is ready for use in only 3,200, according to a government report.</p>

<p>In comparison, some 80 percent of China's villages are linked by broadband.</p>

<p>In Alwar district in the northern state of Rajasthan, many remember when Zuckerberg came to visit but fewer know about Free Basics.</p>

<p>"I've heard it's free and by Facebook and you don't have to pay for it," said Umer Farukh, 43, a folk musician. "But I don't think Facebook should control it. The Internet should be for everybody."</p>

<p>Farukh has only been computer literate for two years, but he's already emailing and using YouTube to post videos and promote his band.</p>

<p>He's become such a proponent that he has donated space for one of Manzar's computer centers ‚Äî part of a government initiative to build cyber-hubs in minority communities ‚Äî and encouraged the female members of his family to take classes, which is rare in his conservative community.</p>

<p>Farukh says that challenges to connecting India go far beyond data plans and fiber-optic cable or the government broadband that often sputters out. Wages are low, and hours are long. Only about half of the women in his state are literate, and about a quarter of the young women in his neighborhood are kept at home and not educated.</p>

<p>"This place is very backward," he said. "India as a society is lagging far behind in terms of Internet."</p>

<p>In the small nearby community of Roja Ka Baas, ringed by fields of blooming mustard greens, residents are still awaiting the opening of their planned WiFi center. They are struggling along on cheap mobile phones with slow 2G spectrum until then, they said.</p>

<p>Sakir Khan, 14, said that once the Internet finally arrived in this village, the first thing he would do would be to sign up for Facebook.</p>

<p>Farheen Fatima and Subuhi Parvez contributed to this report.</p>

## Context and Background

This report documented a critical juncture when Facebook's Free Basics faced regulatory suspension in India and an outright ban in Egypt, signalling that developing countries would not necessarily accept zero-rated platforms as a substitute for genuine internet infrastructure. The timing proved significant: TRAI's suspension came amidst a regulatory consultation that would ultimately produce India's differential pricing prohibition, whilst Egypt's ban demonstrated that pushback extended beyond India's particular policy debates.

Osama Manzar's sense of betrayal captured a broader feeling amongst digital inclusion advocates who had initially welcomed corporate engagement in connectivity challenges, only to discover that "free internet" meant curated access to a handful of services rather than genuine web connectivity. The gap between Zuckerberg's rhetoric about basic human rights and the reality of a Facebook-centred walled garden fuelled accusations that commercial imperatives were dressed up as philanthropy.

Sunil Abraham's characterisation of India's proceedings as a test case proved prescient. TRAI's eventual February 2016 ruling against differential pricing established a regulatory precedent that influenced debates in other jurisdictions grappling with zero-rating and platform power. His observation about "valuable eyeballs to sell to a political party or corporation" pointed to business model concerns that transcended narrow net neutrality arguments‚ÄîFree Basics users generated data and attention that could be monetised through advertising or political targeting, creating asymmetries of power even if the service itself remained nominally free.

The article's rural vignettes illustrated connectivity challenges that made Facebook's pitch attractive despite its limitations. Villages awaiting fibre-optic cable that might never arrive, 2G mobile networks straining to deliver basic functionality, and structural barriers around literacy and gender equality all created conditions where any connectivity could seem better than none. Yet residents like Umer Farukh intuitively grasped the principle at stake: "The Internet should be for everybody," not mediated through a single corporate gatekeeper deciding which services counted as essential.

The comparison to China's broadband rollout underscored how infrastructure gaps shaped these debates. Countries with robust public investment in universal connectivity faced different choices than those relying primarily on private-sector provisioning. India's sluggish progress connecting village centres‚Äî3,200 operational out of 250,000 planned‚Äîcreated space for platform-led initiatives to position themselves as practical alternatives to government programmes, even as critics warned about the long-term costs of allowing platforms to define what counted as internet access for millions of first-time users.
