## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>On Twitter, there's a whole conversation around Facebook Free Basics and whether zero-rating platforms should be allowed in India. Here's a look at the debate.</p>

<hr class="article-separator">

<p>Facebook's Free Basics app, which aims to provide 'free Internet access' to users who can't afford data packs, has run into trouble in India over the last two weeks. After regulator TRAI issued a paper questioning the fairness of zero-rating platforms, it also asked Reliance Communications (the official telecom partner for Free Basics) to put the service on hold.</p>

<p>Facebook on its part has gone for an aggressive campaign, both online and offline, to promote Free Basics and ensure that its platform is not banned permanently. For Net Neutrality activists, zero-rating platforms are in violation of the principle as it restricts access to free, full Internet for users.</p>

<p>On Twitter too, there's a serious debate unfolding around Free Basics and whether zero-rating platforms should be allowed in India. Here's a look at some of the prominent voices around this Net Neutrality vs Free Basics debate.</p>

<p>Nikhil Pahwa, founder of news website MediaNama, has been campaigning for quite some time against zero-rating platforms in general and Net Neutrality. On Twitter, Pahwa points out that the problem with the zero-rating apps is that it gives telcos right to play kingmaker, and get into a direct relationship between a website and a user.</p>

<p>Pahwa also wrote a counter-blog to Mark Zuckerberg's column in The Times of India questioning why Facebook is going with this restricted version of the web on Free Basics, rather than giving access to all websites.</p>

<p>He posted recently on Twitter, "Why hasn't Facebook tried any model other than one which gives it a competitive advantage?"</p>

<p>Pahwa adds, "With zero rating, telcos insert themselves into a previously direct relationship between a site and user. Some sites made cheaper versus others. Said it earlier, saying it again. Problem with zero rating is that it gives telcos the right to play kingmaker through pricing. So Net Neutrality battle isn't just about Facebook. It's about telcos lobbying for differential pricing+revenue share from Internet companies."</p>

<p>Misra has questioned Facebook on what they will do with personal information collected with Free Basics, whether they would allow a Google or Twitter to join the platform. He's also asked if the service will be restricted only to those who can't afford Internet data packs.</p>

<p>Pranesh Prakash, the director for policy at Centre for Internet and Society (CIS) in Bangalore, has said that a total ban might not be the ideal solution and one should look at the platforms on a case by case basis.</p>

<p>He writes on Twitter, "My position: We should ban some zero-rating, allow some zero-rating, and deal w/ middle category either w/ +ve obligation or case-by-case. I'm all for banning Free Basics if it harms people more than it benefits them. I've even proposed tests for determining this. The regulator needs more data on a) conversion rates to full-Internet; b) cost of subsidy & c) QoE (speed, etc.) of Free Basics."</p>

## Context and Background

This article captured a crucial moment in India's net neutrality debate during December 2015. Facebook had launched an aggressive campaign to defend Free Basics, a zero-rating platform offering limited free internet access through Reliance Communications. The Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI) had issued a consultation paper questioning such differential pricing models, prompting Facebook to mobilise public support whilst regulators deliberated.

The debate fractured along multiple lines. Net neutrality advocates like Nikhil Pahwa and Sunil Abraham argued that zero-rating gave telecom operators gatekeeping power and violated equal internet access principles. Critics like Vishal Misra questioned Facebook's data practices and whether the service genuinely benefited underserved populations. Facebook's Michael Buckley defended the platform by citing conversion rates to full internet usage and positioning it as a public-private partnership model.

Pranesh Prakash from the Centre for Internet and Society offered a middle path, suggesting case-by-case regulatory assessment rather than blanket bans. This multi-sided Twitter conversation reflected broader tensions between digital inclusion goals and net neutrality principles. At the time of publication, regulators, companies, and civil society actors were still debating whether zero-rating could coexist with net neutrality, with no settled regulatory outcome.
