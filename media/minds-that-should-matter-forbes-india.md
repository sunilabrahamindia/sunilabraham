<p>Thinkers who best explain a rapidly-changing India to the world (and the world to India)</p>

<hr class="article-separator">

<p>Forbes India wanted me to list India-based intellectuals who are well regarded outside India. This isn't that. Instead, this is a list of smart thinkers and doers who Iâ€”and a few others whose judgement I trustâ€”think ought to be closely followed and read to get a smarter understanding of today's India and its place in the world.</p>

<p>All lists have biases. Let me spell out mine upfront.</p>

<p>In a country where the largest news organisation has led a general dumbing down of public discourse over the years, a key to getting a more nuanced understanding of India is to try and mostly avoid India's many oft-trotted out columnists and TV pundits, with very few exceptions. The depth and quality of established public discourse from India is more opinions and anecdotes than a quality discussion.</p>

<p>The best thoughtfulness often stems from experience. But in the highly personalised, politicised 'he said/he said' abusive debate that passes off as spirited discourse, lack of accepted individual rights and a scarily slow legal system have led to self-censorship among many who have good ideas.</p>

<p>Since I was asked to focus on those living in India, it excludes some very smart thinkers such as economist Gita Gopinath at Harvard and Carnegie Endowment's Milan Vaishnav, one of the best young political scientists of Indian origin whose writing is usually based on previous empirical work.</p>

<p>So here is a list of 24, in no particular order.</p>

<p><strong>Pratap Bhanu Mehta</strong></p>

<p>For a consummate New Delhi insider, he has emerged as the most respected political commentator in India with his habitually centrist perceptive views from his perch as president of Centre for Policy Research.</p>

<p><strong>Ajay Shah</strong></p>

<p>One of the most influential policy economists of a new generation in India. A sharp mind, but one that comes with a combative style that gets him about as many enemies as fans.</p>

<p><strong>Jay Panda</strong></p>

<p>Representing a regional party, Jay is the new face of Indian politics: Young, articulate, liberal and quite vocal about policy issues, ranging from parliamentary reform to campaign finance, and a clear voice of the youth in India.</p>

<p><strong>Niranjan Rajadhyaksha</strong></p>

<p>Lucid, analytical, sparse writing on economics and policy that challenges you to engage with facts and empirical assertions than ideology and conventional wisdom. A must-read.</p>

<p><strong>Meena Kandasamy</strong></p>

<p>A radical, feminist poetess who speaks for Dalits, her writings are more introspective, yet give a good sense of the new thinking in India's oppressed castes.</p>

<p><strong>Nachiket Mor</strong></p>

<p>Gave up one of the best banking jobs to pursue his passion for rural development. Knows more about the changing face of rural India and financial inclusion than most contemporaries.</p>

<p><strong>Ram Madhav</strong></p>

<p>An RSS intellectual who has become very influential in the new BJP regime, he represents the more thoughtful wing of the Hindutva movement. He is ready and willing to engage with ideological opponents.</p>

<p><strong>Jayprakash Narayan</strong></p>

<p>A failed politician, yet perhaps the most thoughtful one in contemporary India. Ignore his electoral debacles, but pay attention to some of the best thinking and speaking on political reform.</p>

<p><strong>Nitin Pai</strong></p>

<p>Runs The Takshashila Institution, a new think tank. One of the new generation's must-read foreign policy thinkers in India, whether you agree with his right-of-centre views or not.</p>

<p><strong>Sunil Abraham</strong></p>

<p>Executive director of The Centre for Internet and Society. Has deep insights into India's rapidly growing digital culture as well as the threats to it from misguided government regulation.</p>

<p><strong>Shuddhabrata Sengupta</strong></p>

<p>Runs Raqs Media Collective and is a founder of the Sarai Collective which does the rare examination of the interplay of urban India/technology/culture.</p>

<p><strong>Anusha Rizvi</strong></p>

<p>The former journalist who directed Peepli Live is now a filmmaker. Peepli was the first ever Indian film to be screened at Sundance. Her response to broadcast media and society issues always make you think.</p>

<p><strong>Mohandas Pai</strong></p>

<p>Ex-Infosys and now with the Manipal Group, he is active in public policy and corporate governance issues, and is not afraid to speak his mind. He was behind the Bangalore Political Action Committeeâ€”first-of-its-kind in Indiaâ€”and is also an activist shareholder who has minority shareholders' interests in mind.</p>

<p><strong>Ramesh Ramanathan</strong></p>

<p>Ex-Citibanker, who heads Janalakshmi, a micro/alternative finance organisation, that has attracted Wall Street money. Offers honest and workable solutions through Janagraha, a hybrid public-private partnership initiative.</p>

<p><strong>Satish Acharya</strong></p>

<p>A brilliant cartoonist from Mangalore. A small-town guy whose views on Indian politics and Indian sport are spot on as he traverses the fine line of cartoons in India: Not too cerebral, but never clichÃ©d and banal either.</p>

<p><strong>Chhavi Rajawat</strong></p>

<p>A young MBA who chose to go back to her ancestral village, Soda in Rajasthan, to help bring management skills to grassroots governance. Won elections to be its sarpanch. A high-profile doer, she will be worth listening to about hands-on governance.</p>

<p><strong>Payal Chawla</strong></p>

<p>While her past claim to fame is taking on Coca-Cola over workplace harassment, as a lawyer and founder of her own law firm, Juscontractus, this University of Chicago alumni would be a good way to track India's troubled legal system.</p>

<p><strong>Pushkar</strong></p>

<p>A professor of Humanities and Social Sciences at BITS Pilani's Goa Campus, he is particularly good on a major challenge for India: Reforming its education system.</p>

<p><strong>Karuna Nundy</strong></p>

<p>A Supreme Court lawyer involved in major commercial and human rights litigation and legal policy, she has contributed in a major way on gender justice in India, recently helping with the new anti-rape laws.</p>

<p><strong>Binalakshmi Nepram</strong></p>

<p>She fights racism against people from the North East and says it like it needs to be said in a country with deep geographical and regional prejudices.</p>

<p><strong>Ireena Vittal</strong></p>

<p>This former McKinsey consultant has a lot of good things to say about smart cities.</p>

<p><strong>Economic and Political Weekly</strong></p>

<p>Ignore its left-leaning interpretations and conclusions. Focus on its outstanding data.</p>

<p><strong>GVL Narasimha Rao</strong></p>

<p>GVL knows his psephology like few others do. His current turn as a spokesman for the BJP yields unrelenting evidence that is often hard to refute. And he takes sides when taking sides can be personally risky.</p>

## Context and Background

This article was published as part of *Forbes India*'s Special section and was explicitly framed as a subjective list. The author makes clear that the selection reflects personal judgement and bias, rather than reputation, popularity, or international recognition.

The piece critiques the state of public discourse in India, arguing that much of it is dominated by repetitive opinion and televised confrontation. Against this backdrop, the list highlights individuals whose thinking and work, in the author's view, offer more grounded and thoughtful ways of engaging with India's social, political, economic, and cultural complexities.

By including figures from across ideological, professional, and disciplinary backgrounds, the article resists a single narrative or consensus. Instead, it positions the list as an invitation to read more widely and engage more deeply with contemporary India, rather than as a definitive ranking of public intellectuals.
