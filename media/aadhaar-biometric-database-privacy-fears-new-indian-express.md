## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>NEW DELHI: Parliament on Wednesday passed legislation that gives central government agencies access to the world's biggest biometric database in the interests of national security, raising fears the privacy of a billion people could be compromised.</p>

<p>The move comes as the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) cracks down on student protests and pushes a Hindu nationalist agenda in assembly elections, steps that some say erode India's traditions of tolerance and free speech.</p>

<p>It could also usher in surveillance far more intrusive than the U.S. telephone and Internet spying revealed by former National Security Agency (NSA) contractor Edward Snowden in 2013, some privacy advocates said.</p>

<p>The Aadhaar database scheme, started seven years ago, was set up to streamline payment of benefits and cut down on massive wastage and fraud, and already nearly a billion people have registered their finger prints and iris signatures.</p>

<p>Now the BJP, which inherited the scheme, has passed new provisions including those on national security, using a loophole to circumvent the opposition in parliament.</p>

<p>"It has been showcased as a tool exclusively meant for disbursement of subsidies and we do not realise that it can also be used for mass surveillance," said Tathagata Satpathy, a lawmaker from Odisha, speaking before the legislation was passed.</p>

<p>"Can the government ... assure us that this Aadhaar card and the data that will be collected under it â€“ biometric, biological, iris scan, finger print, everything put together â€“ will not be misused as has been done by the NSA in the U.S.?"</p>

<p>Finance Minister Arun Jaitley has defended the legislation in parliament, saying Aadhaar saved the government an estimated 150 billion rupees in the 2014-15 financial year alone.</p>

<p>A finance ministry spokesman added that the government had taken steps to ensure citizens' privacy would be respected and the authority to access data was exercised only in rare cases.</p>

<p>According to another government official, the new law is in fact more limited in scope than the decades-old Indian Telegraph Act, which permits national security agencies and tax authorities to intercept telephone conversations of individuals in the interest of public safety.</p>

<p><strong>"POLICE STATE"</strong></p>

<p>Those assurances have not satisfied political opponents and people from religious minorities, including India's sizeable Muslim community, who say the database could be used as a tool to silence them.</p>

<p>"We are midwifing a police state," said Asaduddin Owaisi, an opposition MP.</p>

<p>Raman Jit Singh Chima, global policy director at Access, an international digital rights organisation, said the Indian law lacked the transparency and oversight safeguards found in Europe or the United States, which last year reformed its bulk telephone surveillance programme.</p>

<p>He pointed to the U.S. Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court, which must approve many surveillance requests made by intelligence agencies, and European data protection authorities as oversight mechanisms not present in the Indian legislation.</p>

<p>The government brought the Aadhaar legislation to the Rajya Sabha on Wednesday in a bid to secure passage before lawmakers go into recess.</p>

<p>To get around its lack of a majority, the BJP presented it as a financial bill, which the Rajya Sabha could not reject. It was returned to the Lok Sabha which passed it, as the ruling party has a majority there.</p>

<p>In its assessment of the measure, New Delhi-based PRS Legislative Research said law enforcement agencies could use someone's Aadhaar number as a link across various datasets such as telephone and air travel records.</p>

<p>That would allow them to recognise patterns of behaviour and detect potential illegal activities.</p>

<p>But it could also lead to harassment of individuals who are identified incorrectly as potential security threats, PRS said.</p>

<p>Sunil Abraham, executive director of the Bengaluru-based Centre for Internet and Society, said Aadhaar created a central repository of biometrics for almost every citizen of the world's most populous democracy that could be compromised.</p>

<p>"Maintaining a central database is akin to getting the keys of every house in Delhi and storing them at a central police station," he said.</p>

<p>"It is very easy to capture iris data of any individual with the use of next generation cameras. Imagine a situation where the police is secretly capturing the iris data of protesters and then identifying them through their biometric records."</p>

## Context and Background

This article documented the parliamentary passage of the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act 2016 during a politically contentious period. The BJP government classified the legislation as a money bill, enabling its passage through the Lok Sabha despite the absence of a majority in the Rajya Sabha, a move opposed by several opposition parties.

Aadhaar originated in 2009 under the previous UPA government as a welfare delivery mechanism, aiming to reduce leakages in subsidy schemes through unique biometric identification. By 2016, nearly a billion residents had enrolled, providing fingerprints and iris scans. The new legislation introduced provisions allowing government agencies to access this biometric data for national security purposes, fundamentally expanding the scheme's original remit.

Sunil Abraham from the Centre for Internet and Society characterised the centralised biometric repository as a vulnerability, using the metaphor of storing every household's keys at a single police station. He warned that law enforcement could covertly capture iris scans at protests using advanced cameras and retrospectively identify participants through database matching. Raman Jit Singh Chima from Access highlighted the absence of judicial oversight mechanisms comparable to the U.S. Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court or European data protection authorities.

Opposition MP Asaduddin Owaisi invoked "police state" rhetoric, reflecting anxieties amongst religious minorities about potential discriminatory surveillance. The article appeared amidst broader debates about civil liberties following campus crackdowns and sedition charges against student activists. The Supreme Court of India would later examine Aadhaar's constitutionality in a landmark 2018 judgement that upheld the scheme whilst imposing restrictions on its mandatory use and private sector access.
