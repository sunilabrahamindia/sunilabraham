## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p><strong>Synopsis</strong><br>
Experts suggest a multinational consensus or treaty that strikes a balance between national security concerns and privacy.</p>

<p>BENGALURU/NEW DELHI: The hacking of SIM-card and digital security services provider Gemalto by American and British spy agencies has raised fears that sensitive communications, by the Indian government and hundreds of domestic companies, may have been at the risk of being spied on.</p>

<p>The Netherlands-based Gemalto was jointly hacked by the US National Security Agency and Britain's Government Communications Headquarters, and encryption keys were stolen to monitor mobile communications, according to a news report published last week.</p>

<p>India's largest telecom vendors including Airtel, Vodafone and Idea Cellular use SIM cards supplied by Gemalto, the world's biggest maker of mobile-phone chips and provider of secure devices such as smart cards and tokens. Online publisher The Intercept in its report named Idea Cellular as one of the networks from which the spy agencies accessed encryption keys.</p>

<p>"Phone calls and text messages by military, government, diplomats, spy corporations and by ordinary citizen of India - all of those get affected by this hack," said Pranesh Prakash, Policy Director at research and advocacy firm Centre for Internet and Society.</p>

<p>The Intercept, which accessed top secret documents provided by NSA whistleblower Edward Snowden, said American and British spies dug into the private communications of Gemalto engineers and other employees to steal encryption keys.</p>

<p>Gemalto provides security services such as two-factor authentication and access management, and has hundreds of clients in India. The company in 2012 said it provided 25 million e-driver's licences and vehicle registration certificates in India that let the government "consolidate driver and vehicle registration information across the population in a central repository".</p>

<p>"We believe that the biggest risk stands for the large number of Vodafone users in the country as the company has deployed Gemalto's Near Field Communication services solutions to provide secure and convenient 'wave and pay' contactless transactions via mobile phone," said Sanchit Vir Gogia, Chief Analyst and Group CEO, Greyhound Research.</p>

<p>"We have no further details of these allegations, which are industry-wide in nature and are not focused on any one mobile operator. We will support industry bodies and Gemalto in their investigations," said a Vodafone spokesperson in an email response.</p>

<p>Emails to Idea and Airtel were unanswered till the time of going to Press.</p>

<p>"Indian operators typically go for cheaper Chinese vendors that are anyway low on security. Among the European SIM vendors, Gemalto has the largest share in India," said a senior mobile services executive, requesting anonymity.</p>

<p>The report on the hack comes at a time when Gemalto was looking to tap the Indian market, including e-governance initiatives. The company in a recent email to ET said it had plans to expand its center of excellence in India to develop multiple products, offer tech support and provide security solutions for the domestic market.</p>

<p>"We take this (breach) very seriously and will devote all resources necessary to fully investigate and understand the scope of such highly sophisticated attacks to obtain SIM card data," a Gemalto spokesperson said. "The target was not Gemalto, per se - it was an attempt to try and cast the widest net possible to reach as many mobile phones as possible."</p>

<p>Initial investigations indicate that SIM products as well as banking cards, passports and other products and platforms are secure, the company said. Gemalto is expected to announce the results of its investigation on Wednesday. British and US spy agencies have been under fire for hacking and spying on citizens after Snowden in mid-2013 began leaking documents that revealed massive surveillance programmes by the two governments. At the time, the Indian government said the NSA was only collecting meta-data and had no access to the actual contents of phone calls or text messages.</p>

<p>Experts suggest a multinational consensus or treaty that strikes a balance between national security concerns and privacy.</p>

<p>"Governments will have to debate this in the United Nations and some kind of rules for surveillance, maybe treaties, are relevant in the future," said Kamlesh Bajaj, Chief Executive at Data Security Council of India. "They shall have to have some kind of a limit to surveillance. They can't be vacuuming all data in the name of finding a needle in the haystack."</p>

<p>Sunil Abraham, Executive Director at Center for Internet and Society, suggested the Indian government should replace proprietary operating systems and Android on phones with pure free software projects, encourage use of virtual private network on phones to carry voice and data traffic, and encrypt voice and data payloads separately.</p>

<p>"When it comes to all the other services provided by Gemalto, the India government should insist that they will do key management on their own. This will also mitigate the compromise of Gemalto's enterprise networks by the NSA," he said.</p>

## Context and Background

This 2015 report emerged from The Intercept's publication of Snowden documents detailing Operation AURORAGOLD, through which NSA and GCHQ penetrated Gemalto's corporate networks between 2010-2011. The breach targeted the world's largest SIM card manufacturer, which supplied chips to 450 wireless network providers globally including India's major carriers. By stealing encryption keys stored on Gemalto servers, intelligence agencies could decrypt mobile communications without requiring cooperation from telecom operators or equipment manufacturers‚Äîa capability that bypassed legal oversight mechanisms entirely.

Pranesh Prakash's warning that "military, government, diplomats" communications were compromised reflected the scope of potential exposure. India's diplomatic corps, defence personnel, and intelligence officers relied on commercial SIM cards from operators using Gemalto chips, meaning their supposedly secure communications may have been transparently accessible to foreign intelligence services for years. The compromise extended beyond voice calls to include Gemalto's work on India's e-governance infrastructure, including 25 million digital driver's licenses stored in centralized repositories.

Sunil Abraham's technical recommendations addressed systemic dependencies on foreign-controlled cryptographic infrastructure. His call for government-controlled key management challenged the standard industry model where manufacturers like Gemalto retained encryption keys for operational convenience. Advocating for open-source operating systems and separate voice-data encryption layers represented efforts to reduce single points of failure that made mass surveillance architecturally feasible. These proposals acknowledged that proprietary systems from Western vendors carried inherent risks when those vendors' home governments operated aggressive signals intelligence programmes.

Gemalto's defensive posture‚Äîclaiming it was targeted incidentally rather than specifically‚Äîunderstated the operation's strategic calculus. Intelligence agencies deliberately chose Gemalto precisely because its market dominance created surveillance leverage across hundreds of networks simultaneously. The company's initial reassurances that "products and platforms are secure" rang hollow given that compromised encryption keys rendered such security meaningless. Kamlesh Bajaj's call for international surveillance treaties recognized that technical countermeasures alone couldn't address the governance vacuum enabling indiscriminate collection justified by elastic national security claims.
