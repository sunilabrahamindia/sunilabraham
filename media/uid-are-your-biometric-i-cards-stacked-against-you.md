## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p><strong>Synopsis</strong><br>
A large number of government departments and private companies are collecting biometric data. But there is little protection against misuse.</p>

<hr class="article-separator">

<p>Imagine a rural family of five. Mom. Dad. Two kids. And Grandma. Assume too that they are below the poverty line. The day is coming when this family will have to give its biometrics out to myriad agencies.</p>

<p>You know that Nandan Nilekani's Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI) or the Registrar General's National Population Register (NPR) has been collecting biometrics for a while now.</p>

<p>But a set of other departments have entered the fray. This ranges from the PDS department, ministry of rural development (MoRD), states' education departments, the Rashtriya Swasthya Bima Yojana (RSBY), banks, the department of social welfare, the post office...they are all collecting biometrics (see Agencies Collecting Biometrics Right Now).</p>

<p>This is the latest iteration in India's tryst with biometrics. From a beginning where only the NPR ‚Äî and, a little later, the UIDAI ‚Äî were to capture biometrics, we have now reached a point where myriad departments and ministries are camping in India's villages and towns, capturing fingerprints and iris images.</p>

<h2>Identity Thieves</h2>

<p>There was to be one large database. Now, we are moving to a system where multiple agencies capture and store biometrics data in myriad servers. This is amplifying the risk of biometric theft.</p>

<p>As Sunil Abraham, the head of Bangalore-based Centre for Internet and Society says, "If biometrics is used as authentication factor then it would be possible for a criminal to harvest your biometrics ‚Äî such as using a glass to collect fingerprints ‚Äî without your conscious cooperation. Or the registrar can cache your biometrics and duplicate transactions."</p>

<p>As the number of databases containing biometrics rises, the risk of this information leaking out increases. There have been complaints against an UIDAI enrolment agency called Madras Security Printers that it had sold data to private companies. There were also charges that enrolment agencies had outsourced the enrolment work to other companies, which they are not allowed to do.</p>

<p>What complicates matters further is there are not many safeguards. The country doesn't have a policy on how biometrics can be captured, used, stored and destroyed. But before we get deeper into that story, it is useful to understand why multiple departments have begun collecting biometrics.</p>

<h2>Biometric Rush</h2>

<p>According to a senior bureaucrat who recently retired from the ministry of planning, the answer lies in the 2014 elections. "For the government, cash transfers are the large reforms that they think UPA 2 can point towards in the next elections. For this reason, they need all this up and running before 2014."</p>

<p>However, over the past few months, parts of the government are increasingly unsure if UIDAI and NPR will meet their targets. "I do not think the 2014 target can be met at all," says a senior official in the National Informatics Centre (NIC). "We have to enroll another 800 million people. Then, we have to deduplicate them. Then, we have to make the cards and distribute them."</p>

<p>This is one reason why a set of government departments are configuring their own alternatives. Take the Department of Financial Services (DFS). It has been testing an online, biometric system for cash payments in Haryana's Mewat district for months now. Here, each bank will store its customers' biometric information in its own servers.</p>

<p>If a customer of bank A goes to a banking correspondent (BC) agent of bank B, his biometrics would be forwarded by bank B to bank A for authentication. Once authenticated, the transaction will be completed. "We should be rolling the new system out nationally from July or August," says the bureaucrat.</p>

<p>The rural development ministry is also testing its payment system. Once the local administration tells the ministry about who worked how many days, the ministry will be able to put money into their accounts automatically via a payment gateway. Right now, this is done manually with the block development officer and sarpanch making out the cheques.</p>

<p>This pilot, says DK Jain, joint secretary, MoRD, started 3-4 months ago in parts of Gujarat, Karnataka, Odisha and Rajasthan. In another six months, it will be available across the country. And then, there is the PDS.</p>

<p>Here, different states are putting different systems in place. Andhra, says a senior mandarin in the food ministry, is going with UID, Haryana is looking at smart cards, Jharkhand is going with Aadhaar, MP and Gujarat are testing food coupons, while Chhattisgarh has decided to use RSBY and Orissa has chosen NPR.</p>

<p>Apart from this, data is also being collected by the RSBY and BC companies on behalf of the banks handling welfare payments, or scrambling to meet their financial inclusion targets.</p>

<h2>A New Set of Worries</h2>

<p>As the number of databases rises, a new set of worrying questions are coming to the fore. The first has to do with this enthusiastic adoption of biometrics. If they do not work, people might be excluded from something as basic as citizenship, or from government programmes.</p>

<p>Second, safety of this information. If your credit card PIN becomes public information, you can always call your bank and get it blocked. But what do you do if someone gets hold of your biometrics?</p>

<p>Says human rights researcher Usha Ramanathan, "Biometrics is intimate personal data. Its proliferation represents a distinct threat to the personal security of the individual. Interestingly, it has hardly been tested, and when tested, been found deeply defective. Biometrics does not work for everyone, it can be stolen, it cannot be replaced, it changes, and none of this is acknowledged. Biometrics is too sensitive to be collected, held, transacted and shared without stringent protection of law."</p>

<p>However, we have rushed ahead. A suggestion from the standing committee on finance which, while rejecting the draft National Identification Authority of India Bill, said biometrics cannot be collected without discussion and authorisation by the parliament has gone entirely ignored.</p>

<h2>Cyber Security</h2>

<p>And then, there are data safety questions. Says the NIC official, "In my opinion if all the solutions are in isolation to each other then there cannot be any common safeguard mechanism. Every organisation shall have to ensure their own data security by applying normal cyber security principles."</p>

<p>The official was referring to technology standards ‚Äî on data encryption and firewalls. How are we doing here? Not very well. Says B Sambamurthy, head of Hyderabad-based Institute for Development and Research into Banking Technology: "There are standards for capturing, storaging and retrieving of biometric data. The problem is not with technology or standards but rigorous compliance."</p>

<p>And then, there are more procedural aspects ‚Äî like ensuring that the information collected is not shared, or that it be used only for the purpose for which it was collected. These are entirely missing. Take Andhra Pradesh, where the government tried to share the biometrics it had collected for one programme with other government departments. But that triggers larger questions about consent and ownership over biometric information. Can a person's biometrics be used in ways he or she has not expressly authorised?</p>

<p>These are issues that the privacy bill will have to look at. Says a bureaucrat working on the bill, "It will lay down the broad standards. Any agency which wants to collect this information will need to get enrolled or registered with a central body before it can start collecting data. It cannot share this data with anyone else. It also lays down the penalties in case anyone violates these terms."</p>

<p>It also envisages the creation of a new agency ‚Äî a standalone agency which will define privacy standards and monitor compliance. But, it is a long way off. The ministry wants to revise the Bill in the coming month, and then place the bill online for public comments, and then another round of interministerial consultations.</p>

<p>In the meantime, be careful. There is little by way of penalties that can be imposed on any organisation that shares your information with anyone.</p>

## Context and Background

This June 2012 article captured critical juncture where India's biometric infrastructure fragmented from centralized UIDAI/NPR vision into proliferating departmental systems creating myriad databases without coordinated safeguards. Anonymous retired planning ministry bureaucrat's observation that "cash transfers are large reforms UPA 2 can point towards in next elections" requiring "all this up and running before 2014" revealed electoral timeline driving biometric rush‚Äîgovernment treating identity infrastructure as deliverable for 2014 general election campaign rather than carefully architected long-term governance transformation.

National Informatics Centre official's skepticism that "2014 target cannot be met at all" requiring enrollment of "another 800 million people, deduplicate them, make cards and distribute them" exposed implementation bottlenecks prompting departmental workarounds. Department of Financial Services testing bank-specific biometric authentication in Haryana's Mewat district, Ministry of Rural Development piloting payment gateways in Gujarat/Karnataka/Odisha/Rajasthan, and states adopting divergent PDS systems‚ÄîAndhra choosing UID, Haryana smart cards, Jharkhand Aadhaar, MP/Gujarat food coupons, Chhattisgarh RSBY, Orissa NPR‚Äîillustrated fragmentation defeating original single-database architecture.

Sunil Abraham's warning that "criminal can harvest your biometrics such as using glass to collect fingerprints without your conscious cooperation" whilst "registrar can cache your biometrics and duplicate transactions" identified fundamental vulnerabilities distinguishing biometrics from revocable credentials. Unlike passwords or PINs changeable after compromise, fingerprints and iris scans constitute immutable biological identifiers whose theft creates permanent security liability. Abraham's dual threat model‚Äîexternal criminals lifting latent fingerprints from surfaces, and insider registrars caching biometric templates for fraudulent replay attacks‚Äîencompassed both opportunistic and systemic attack vectors.

Madras Security Printers allegations of selling UIDAI enrollment data to private companies alongside charges that enrollment agencies outsourced work to unauthorized subcontractors demonstrated governance failures even within supposedly controlled UIDAI ecosystem. If primary enrollment agency violated data protection whilst regulatory oversight failed detecting breaches, proliferation to dozens of departments and banks with weaker institutional capacity exponentially amplified risks. Each additional database created honeypot target whilst multiplying insider threat surface area across thousands of enrollment agents and banking correspondents.

Usha Ramanathan's characterization of biometrics as "intimate personal data" whose "proliferation represents distinct threat to personal security" whilst "hardly been tested and when tested been found deeply defective" challenged technical solutionism undergirding identity projects. Her litany‚Äî"does not work for everyone, can be stolen, cannot be replaced, changes, and none of this acknowledged"‚Äîenumerated exclusion risks (manual laborers with worn fingerprints), security risks (irreversible compromise), and aging risks (biometric drift over decades) systematically ignored in implementation rush. Observation that "biometrics too sensitive to be collected, held, transacted and shared without stringent protection of law" positioned legal safeguards not as nice-to-have procedural formalities but existential prerequisites for legitimate biometric deployment.

Standing committee on finance rejection of draft National Identification Authority of India Bill with stipulation that "biometrics cannot be collected without discussion and authorization by parliament" being "entirely ignored" represented constitutional crisis. Executive branch proceeding with mass biometric collection after legislative branch explicitly withheld authorization inverted parliamentary supremacy‚Äîtreating elected representatives' concerns as advisory rather than binding. This procedural violation prefigured later Supreme Court interventions where executive Aadhaar expansion through executive orders and money bills circumvented ordinary legislative scrutiny.

Anonymous NIC official's assessment that "if all solutions are in isolation to each other then there cannot be any common safeguard mechanism" whilst "every organisation shall have to ensure their own data security by applying normal cyber security principles" acknowledged coordination failure whilst normalizing it. Treating biometric data security as ordinary IT security problem addressable through "normal principles" understated unique stakes‚Äîcompromised database credentials enable password resets whilst compromised biometric databases create permanent identity theft vulnerabilities. B Sambamurthy's observation that "problem is not with technology or standards but rigorous compliance" identified implementation gap between formal security protocols and actual organizational practices.

Andhra Pradesh government attempting to share biometrics collected for one programme with other departments whilst bureaucrat acknowledged this "triggers larger questions about consent and ownership over biometric information" illustrated function creep absent legal constraints. Collection under specific welfare program pretext followed by repurposing for unrelated government functions without renewed consent demonstrated how biometric databases become general-purpose surveillance infrastructure‚Äîinitial narrow justifications expanding to encompass any imaginable government interest once collection infrastructure exists.

Privacy bill provisions requiring agencies "get enrolled or registered with central body before collecting data" whilst prohibiting sharing with "anyone else" and establishing penalties for violations remained vaporware. Bureaucrat's timeline‚Äî"ministry wants to revise Bill in coming month, place online for public comments, then another round of interministerial consultations"‚Äîindicated years-long legislative process whilst biometric collection proceeded immediately. Warning that "there is little by way of penalties that can be imposed on any organisation that shares your information with anyone" acknowledged current legal vacuum‚Äîmass biometric collection occurring in regulatory no-man's-land where violations carried no meaningful consequences.
