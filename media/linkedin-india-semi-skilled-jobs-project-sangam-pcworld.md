**LinkedIn Will Help People in India Train for Semi-Skilled Jobs** is a *PCWorld* article by John Ribeiro, published on 21 February 2017. The report covers Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella's announcement of Project Sangam, a LinkedIn-integrated cloud service aimed at skilling and employing low- and semi-skilled workers in India, alongside Microsoft's broader integration with the Aadhaar biometric system. [Sunil Abraham](/sunil/), then Executive Director of the Centre for Internet and Society, is quoted criticising Microsoft's support for Aadhaar as support for a centralized surveillance project of the Indian government.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p><em>Microsoft is addressing the low- and semi-skilled jobs market with its new Project Sangam</em></p>

<p>Microsoft has launched Project Sangam, a cloud service integrated with LinkedIn that will help train and generate employment for middle and low-skilled workers.</p>

<p>The professional network that was acquired by Microsoft in December has been generally associated with educated urban professionals but the company is now planning to extend its reach to semi-skilled people in India.</p>

<p>Having connected white-collared professionals around the world with the right job opportunities and training through LinkedIn Learning, the platform is now developing a new set of products that extends this service to low- and semi-skilled workers, said Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella at an event on digital transformation in Mumbai on Wednesday.</p>

<p>Project Sangam, which is in private preview, is "the first project that is now the coming together of LinkedIn and Microsoft, where we are building this cloud service with deep integration with LinkedIn, so that we can start tackling that enormous challenge in front of us of how to provide every person in India the opportunity to skill themselves for the jobs that are going to be available."</p>

<p>LinkedIn also has a placement product for college graduates that will help students find jobs regardless of whether they studied at top universities or not, Nadella added.</p>

<p>Microsoft announced earlier in the day its Skype Lite, a version of Skype that consumes less data. The company offers since September a 'lite' version of LinkedIn, reflecting the need for vendors to factor in low Internet bandwidth, usually running on low-cost and inadequately featured smartphones, when designing products for markets in countries like India.</p>

<p>LinkedIn Lite works on 2G links and is four times faster than the original LinkedIn client, Nadella said.</p>

<p>A large number of low-skilled and semi-skilled workers that Microsoft is targeting with its Sangam project still use feature phones, which will likely be a challenge as Microsoft tries to popularize the service.</p>

<p>Nadella has also backed a controversial Indian government sponsored project to use biometric data collected from over 1 billion people as an authentication mechanism for a variety of services offered by both the government and the private sector.</p>

<p>The project, called India Stack, aims to use a biometric system, called Aadhaar, to facilitate the digital exchange of information. Microsoft said on Tuesday that Skype Lite would support Aadhaar authentication, pointing out to potential uses of the technology such as for verifying the identity of a candidate for a video job interview. Project Sangam too offers authentication using Aadhaar.</p>

<p>Skype Lite is another example of how India Stack is driving the company's innovation agenda, Nadella said in Mumbai. He announced in Bangalore on Monday that the company's end user products including Windows would be "great participants in the India Stack."</p>

<p>The Aadhaar project has been criticized by privacy activists for collecting biometric information such as the fingerprints and iris scans of people in a central database, which could be misused by both governments and hackers who might get access to the data. The government has been trying to extend the use of Aadhaar, initially designed for the distribution of government benefits and subsidies, to a variety of financial and other services.</p>

<p>"It is indeed shameful that Microsoft is supporting the centralized surveillance project of the Indian government which has dramatically increased the fragility of the Indian information society," said Sunil Abraham, executive director of Bangalore-based research organization, the Centre for Internet and Society.</p>

<p>"As Indian citizens we must realize that Microsoft will have our biometrics or our authentication factors that can be used to frame us in crimes or clean out our bank accounts," he added.</p>

## Context and Background

The article reflects a broader shift by technology companies towards addressing employment and skills gaps in emerging markets. Microsoft's Project Sangam, integrated with LinkedIn, was positioned as an attempt to extend digital job matching and training services beyond urban, highly educated users to include low- and semi-skilled workers.

This expansion required adapting products to local conditions, including limited bandwidth, widespread use of feature phones, and fragmented access to formal training systems. Initiatives such as LinkedIn Lite and Skype Lite were part of this approach, aiming to make services accessible on low-cost devices and networks.

At the same time, the integration of Aadhaar-based authentication into these services brought the project into ongoing debates about biometric identity systems in India. While such integration promised streamlined verification and service delivery, it also raised concerns among critics about centralisation of sensitive data, surveillance risks, and the adequacy of legal and technical safeguards.
