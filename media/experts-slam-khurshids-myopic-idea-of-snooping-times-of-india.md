## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>NEW DELHI: External affairs minister Salman Khurshid's view that the recently-revealed US surveillance of the Indian embassy is "scrutiny", and not "snooping" has been questioned by cyber security experts and activists.</p>

<p>Mumbai-based professor of law Saurav Datta points out that scrutiny and snooping can't be so easily segregated. "To say that scrutiny does not involve surveillance would be myopic. In the name of security, a lot of civil rights violations are carried out, as we have seen recently. Surveillance will be an indispensable part of cyber security measures. And all this is covert. Anyone leaking information is liable for prosecution. Just as it happened with Gen (Retd.) Cartwright of the US, who was a part of the Stuxnet programme," says Datta. Stuxnet is a cyber "worm" that was used to attack an alleged Iranian nuclear facility. It is said to have been developed jointly by the US and Israel.</p>

<p>Datta also feels that the time is ripe to revive the PIL where a former DU law faculty dean appealed to the Supreme Court to seek action against the US accessing Indian users' data. "Now that there is cogent evidence of the NSA and Internet giants' surveillance over the Indian embassy, there are strong grounds for the Supreme Court to recall and restore the PIL against PRISM which it had dismissed last week."</p>

<p>Khurshid said that "only" patterns of calls and messages were studied (that is, metadata) and not the actual content of messages. That too is a reason for worry, say experts.</p>

<p>"Metadata is bad enough. There have been many researches on how such collection of metadata is also harmful and violative. Even the closest allies of the US have been very vocal in their criticism of this snooping," says Parminder Jeet Singh, executive director, IT for Change, a Bangalore-based NGO involved in research and advocacy.</p>

<p>Payload data — in this case, data that gives you the actual transmitted content rather than the time and frequency of it — is "only the icing on the metadata", says Sunil Abraham, executive director, Center for Internet and Society. He cites the example of American businessman Rajat Gupta, who was charged with insider trading, though the transcripts of his calls had nothing to implicate him.</p>

<p>"Wiretapping standards used to require executive authorization for metadata and judicial authorization for payload data which is considered more sensitive. But now we realize that it is metadata which proves to be more useful. Rajat Gupta wasn't convicted because of the content of his calls. It was the timing and frequency of calls to Rajaratnam and the timing of trade that got him. Metadata is used to determine the calls you will pick for payload data. To say that scanning of metadata does not result in snooping is a useless statement to make," says Abraham.</p>

<p>On Sunday, British newspaper The Guardian reported that data and documents leaked by US whistleblower Edward Snowden showed that the US had been spying on 38 embassies and missions, including India. The EU, especially France and Germany, have taken a strong stance against this snooping.</p>

## Context and Background

The article was published days after The Guardian revealed, on the basis of documents leaked by Edward Snowden, that the US National Security Agency had been conducting surveillance on 38 embassies and missions including India's. The disclosure prompted a political row in India, with External Affairs Minister Salman Khurshid seeking to minimise the severity of the surveillance by drawing a distinction between "scrutiny" and "snooping."

Sunil Abraham's argument that metadata is at least as revealing as content drew on the then-recent conviction of Rajat Gupta in the United States, where call timing and frequency rather than call content formed the basis of the insider trading case. The disclosures also drew criticism from several countries, particularly in Europe, reflecting wider concern about the scope of US surveillance.
