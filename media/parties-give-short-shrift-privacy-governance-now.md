**Parties Give Short Shrift to Privacy** is an analytical piece published in *Governance Now* on 12 April 2014. Written ahead of India's general election, the article examines how major political parties addressed digital rights, privacy legislation, and technology governance in their manifestos. The piece highlights significant omissions in both the Congress and BJP documents whilst noting the CPI-M's relatively detailed engagement with cyber security and civil liberties issues.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>Both the Congress and BJP vision documents disappoint, but the real surprise is the CPI-M document that deals with cyber issues in a substantial manner.</p>

<hr class="article-separator">

<p>For civil rights activists in the internet and cyber space, the election manifestoes of major political parties including the Congress and the BJP have come as a disappointment. Both the parties are mute on privacy. In the recent past there has been a vociferous demand for a strong legislation on privacy. A draft bill on privacy has been making rounds of the bureaucratic circle for three years. Manifestoes are also silent on the need for correction in the information technology act, which activists say is characterised by 'arbitrariness and lack of processes'.</p>

<p>"A healthy democracy gives equal weightage to transparency and privacy. It's disappointing that the two parties have overlooked these two," says Sunil Abraham, director of the Bangalore based Centre for Internet and Society (CIS). Both Congress and BJP don't mention the lack of implementation of the open data policy. The policy, aka NDSAP 2012, requires all departments and ministries to put high value data sets in public domain within a few months of the policy enforcement. The parties are also silent on need for a balancing act on surveillance and civil liberty.</p>

<p>Nikhil Pahwa, founder of Medianama.com, a portal posting news and analysis on digital media, says "The parties could have talked about reforming the IT legislation, especially the Section 79 and IT Rules 2011 which gives the intermediariesâ€”the ISPs, websites, and cyber cafesâ€”the power to strike down content without even hearing the author." The law, currently, doesn't provide a redressal mechanism to the author.</p>

<p>Similarly both parties are mute on internet governance, which has become a major global issue after the US showed willingness to cede its monopolistic oversight over the body governing the internet ICANN.</p>

<p>The Congress manifesto is also blank on making websites and systems accessible for specially-abled population, also called as e-accessibility. While the BJP too doesn't talk about making government portals e-accessible, it speaks about the use of technology to deliver low cost quality education to specially-abled students. Issuance of universal identity cards for all applicable government benefits and disabled friendly access to public facilities are two other things which the party promises to implement if voted in power.</p>

<p>Both election manifestoes don't mention concerns related to the telecommunication sector. Broadband is the only term that appears in the two manifestoes. The Congress promises to bring high speed Internet to every village panchayat. This is not a new initiative; a project under DoT called national optical fibre network, NOFN, proposes to do the same. The BJP's manifesto says, "Deployment of broadband in every village would be a thrust area."</p>

<p>Both parties also talk about putting public services online. There is also nothing concrete about promotion of indigenous manufacturing in electronics and IT hardware. While there are serious omissions in the two manifestoes, the manifesto of the CPI-M surprises many, highlighting key issues concerning civil rights and liberty.</p>

<p>The manifesto talks about 'demilitarisation of cyber space' and 'protecting Internet and telecommunications networks from cyber attacks and surveillance by building indigenous capability'. Edward Snowden's revelation of the PRISM programme seems to be the context. It also talks about promoting 'free software and other such new technologies which are free from monopoly ownership through copyrights or patents; knowledge commons should be promoted across disciplines, like biotechnology and drug discovery'.</p>

<p>Without elaboration, the BJP also highlighted open source, open standard software and digital and cyber security in its manifesto. 'Information technology for good governance' and the Internet have been dealt in a couple of lines in Aam Aadmi Party's manifesto. "We will promote use of IT to promote transparency and reduce corruption in government functioning," says the AAP manifesto.</p>

</div>

## Context and Background

This article appeared weeks before India's 2014 general election, which took place across nine phases between 7 April and 12 May 2014. The piece analysed how major political parties framed digital governance, privacy protection, and technology policy in their election documents during a period of heightened global attention to surveillance practices.

The Edward Snowden revelations of mid-2013 had exposed extensive mass surveillance programmes operated by the United States National Security Agency, including the targeting of Indian communications. These disclosures sparked debates within India about both foreign surveillance of Indian citizens and the absence of adequate legal safeguards constraining domestic intelligence gathering.

At the time of publication, a draft privacy bill had circulated within government departments for approximately three years without advancement. The National Data Sharing and Accessibility Policy (NDSAP-2012), approved in early 2012, mandated government departments to release high-value datasets publicly, yet implementation remained patchy. Section 79 of the Information Technology Act and the accompanying IT Rules 2011 empowered intermediaries to remove content without providing affected authors with recourse mechanisms, drawing criticism from digital rights advocates.

The article noted the CPI-M manifesto's engagement with issues such as cyber space demilitarisation, indigenous capability building against surveillance, and promotion of free software and knowledge commons. This contrasted with the limited treatment these topics received in the Congress and BJP documents, which focused primarily on broadband expansion and e-governance without addressing structural questions about privacy legislation, surveillance oversight, or internet governance reform.
