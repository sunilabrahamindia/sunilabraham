**Activists Bemoan Absence of 'Active' Pirate Party in India** is a *The Times of India* report by Kim Arora, published on 2 May 2014. It examines why attempts to form an active Pirate Party in India have stalled, and quotes [Sunil Abraham](/sunil/) on how low internet penetration and the underdeveloped state of IP rights and privacy as policy issues make it difficult for a Pirate Party agenda to gain mainstream traction in India.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>NEW DELHI: As the 16th Lok Sabha elections enter a decisive phase, social media continues to pay a vital part in mobilizing public opinion nationwide. But online activists and bloggers bemoan the absence of "an active" Pirate Party in India which could have become a political agency for critical internet issues such as digital freedom and web censorship.</p>

<p>The concept of Pirate Party originated in Sweden and it gets its name from a Swedish NGO Piratbyran, the NGO behind file-sharing torrent website The Pirate Bay. Piratbyran is against intellectual property. In 2006, the Swedish Pirate Party contested on the plank of defending privacy rights and reforming copyright and patent laws. In the 2009 elections in Sweden, they got over seven per cent of the total votes. Pirate Party Australia, registered in 2013, also focuses on these issues in addition to internet censorship and digital rights. The Pirate Party UK contested its first election in 2010. Around 60 countries have Pirate Parties today, with a widespread popularity in Europe.</p>

<p>In 2012, Praveen Arimbrathodiyil, a techie-turned-activist from Pune tried to establish a "Pirate Party of India" with about five others. Borrowing from the global phenomenon of pirate parties, the political outfit was to focus on free knowledge, transparent government and digital freedoms.</p>

<p>Arimbrathodiyil says that while the talk around forming a party had been going on for about three to four years, lack of coordination among participants got the better of it. Today it is an online group of about 30 active members. "Some people were impatient to register while some of us wanted to build a base of supporters first," says the 30-year-old, who has been holding workshops on privacy, data security and the monopolization of the social networking industry in Kerala's colleges.</p>

<p>Sunil Abraham, executive director of the Bangalore-based research organization Centre for Internet and Society, says that low internet penetration and a limited appeal of issues typically associated with the Pirate Party could have been the deal breakers in India. "In countries like Germany and Sweden, internet penetration is a lot higher than in India. Digital and online freedoms are still not a mainstream issue. Other areas like intellectual property rights and privacy are not yet developed policy areas in India," says Abraham. Internet penetration in India stood at 12% last year. Sweden by comparison, stands at 94%.</p>

<p>Mumbai-based blogger and activist Vidyut Kale became active with the Indian Pirate Party last year; its members connected only by the Internet. "They weren't focused on mainstream politics per se, but more into issues like, say, free and open source software," recalls Kale, who says that those associated with the Pirate Party of India have not given up the idea completely. "It's still at the back of everyone's mind," says Kale, adding that in the face of increased online surveillance by governments, the pirate party agenda remains relevant.</p>

<p>Low awareness is still something they are trying to battle, Arimbrathodiyil says. "Most people still don't think of online freedoms as a right. There is still a lot of fear about the internet, in terms of data theft and other issues. There is still a long way to go," he says.</p>

<p>In the last two years, the debate on online freedoms and censorship has intensified. Cartoonist Aseem Trivedi's website "cartoons against corruption" was taken down in September 2012. In November that year, two Palghar college students were arrested for a Facebook post protesting the state-wide bandh that followed the death of Shiv Sena supremo Bal Thackeray. A Jadavpur University professor Ambikesh Mahapatra was arrested for lampooning Bengal chief minister Mamata Banerjee online.</p>

<p>Rajya Sabha MP Rajeev Chandrasekhar, formerly a software engineer, feels that the discourse on internet freedom has not reached the critical mass for it to become a mainstream movement. "The issues that the pirate parties address are sophisticated. There may not be space for a non-mainstream party, but there is space for these issues in the agendas of mainstream political parties today," says Chandrasekhar.</p>

<p>Both Kale and Chandrasekhar find resonances of the pirate party movement in the Aam Aadmi Party. Kale says she is trying to urge AAP through Twitter to adopt certain issues like privacy rights, which are integral to pirate parties across the world. "Both the parties were born out of a protest, have crowdsourced their manifestos and touched a common sentiment," says Kale. "AAP is the closest that comes to it (Pirate Party)," says Chandrasekhar. As for Arimbrathodiyil, he says that personally he supports AAP. "They too seem to believe in the idea of participative democracy, which is something the pirate party also endorses," he says.</p>

## Context and Background

The article was published on 2 May 2014, during the final stages of India's 16th Lok Sabha general election, a period marked by increased social media use and growing attention to issues of online speech, surveillance, and platform power.

The Pirate Party movement globally had its roots in opposition to aggressive copyright enforcement and state surveillance. By 2014 it had elected members to the European Parliament and held seats in several national legislatures. India's attempt to form one ran into the structural barriers Sunil Abraham identified: with internet penetration at roughly 12% against Sweden's 94%, the issues that animate Pirate Parties globally simply had not yet become salient for a large enough share of Indian voters or civic activists.

The censorship incidents cited in the article, including the arrests over social media posts in 2012, were among the triggers that pushed digital rights into Indian public discourse.
