**Panama Papers Law Firm Founder Says Massive Offshore Company Leak Is 'Campaign Against Privacy'. We Disagree.** is a Privacy International news and analysis piece published on 4 April 2016, the day after the Panama Papers revelations were published. The piece responds to Mossack Fonseca co-founder Ramon Fonseca's framing of the leak as an attack on privacy, arguing instead that privacy and transparency are complementary rather than opposing values. [Sunil Abraham](/sunil/), then executive director of the Centre for Internet and Society, is quoted making the case that the right to privacy should be inversely proportionate to power, while the requirement for transparency should be directly proportionate to it.

## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>We do agree with Ramon Fonseca about one thing: that "Each person has a right to privacy, whether they are a king or a beggar."</p>

<p>But that's where our commonality with co-founder of disgraced Panama law firm Mossack Fonseca ends.</p>

<p>Last year, a whistleblower leaked 11.5 million documents about the firm's business brokering offshore companies, details of which were published yesterday. Reportedly the largest leak in journalistic history, the cache reveals hidden assets by a dozen current and former world leaders, and scores of celebrities and tycoons, some of which are linked to high level corruption scandals.</p>

<p>This scandal isn't about privacy, though. If anything, it's about the need for transparency about how the powerful wield their power. We need transparency — and good solid investigation — to understand where and how our right to privacy is eroded.</p>

<p>Privacy and transparency are not opposites. They are two sides of the same coin. As privacy advocates, we use transparency capabilities to investigate surveillance. Meanwhile, privacy as a right requires transparency from the institutions that gather and use our data.</p>

<p>Privacy International, like other human rights groups, conducts investigations in the public interest. That allows us to understand, for example, how Colombia built a shadow surveillance system despite evidence of illegal interceptions, or how UK police appear to be collecting private communications data at protests, according to a Vice News investigation.</p>

<p>Many of the Panama Papers' revelations are in the public interest insofar as they concern the transformation of public assets — like taxpayers' money and state funds — into private gains, and allow the powerful to avoid scrutiny.</p>

<p>Fonseca called journalism around the leaked files an "international campaign against privacy".</p>

<p>But what Fonseca is really doing is advocating a status quo of 'privacy for the kings, and transparency for the beggars'. Or rather, privacy for the business moguls, politicians, corporations and government agencies, and transparency for the citizens, consumers, activists and journalists.</p>

<p>For the public, our financial systems are now surveiled by design. Our transactions are labelled as suspicious and sent for mining by intelligence agencies. We need IDs to open accounts, and our records are profiled by credit agencies who facilitate key decisions about us and our families. Secretive institutions collate this information to decide whether or not we are terrorists. While a certain degree of this is necessary for public order, what's clear is that we are watched while the 'kings' are able to circumvent many of these measures and escape scrutiny. We should never make the mistake of conflating the right to privacy for the individual with the desire to hide shadowy, ethically dubious, borderline-or-actual illegal activity for the immensely wealthy and powerful.</p>

<p>The real issues around privacy include: the spreading of draconian laws, from the UK, to Pakistan, to Kenya, that sanction warrantless surveillance and online monitoring, with insufficient protection for the public; it's in the intrusive biometric registration of some of the most desperate people, like refugees from Dadaab to Calais, desperate for food and medical care; it's in the instrumentalisation of consumer data to draw conclusions about us, with or without our consent; it's also in the parallel trend of rolling back Freedom of Information laws (see: UK and United States); and, as the Panama Papers show, allowing transfers of public funds for private gain to be obscured.</p>

<p>That is the real "campaign against privacy" — not public interest journalism.</p>

<p>As our friend and partner Sunil Abraham, Executive Director of the Centre for Internet and Society in India <a href="/publications/privacy-law-must-fit-the-bill/">states succinctly</a>, the right to privacy should "be inversely proportionate to power and almost conversely the requirement of transparency to be directly proportionate to power."</p>

</div>

## Context and Background

The Panama Papers were published on 3 April 2016 by a consortium of investigative journalists coordinated by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists, based on 11.5 million documents leaked from Mossack Fonseca, a Panamanian law firm that specialised in incorporating offshore shell companies. The revelations implicated dozens of heads of state, public officials, and prominent figures across multiple countries in the concealment of assets.

Ramon Fonseca's response was to characterise the publication of the leaked documents as an assault on the privacy of his clients. Privacy International's piece directly contested that framing, drawing a distinction between the privacy rights of individuals and the opacity claimed by the powerful to avoid accountability.

Sunil Abraham's [quoted formulation](/publications/privacy-law-must-fit-the-bill/), that privacy should be inversely proportionate to power while transparency should be directly proportionate to it, captures a principle that runs through much of his public advocacy during this period. It treats privacy not as a uniform right applied equally to all parties but as a structural protection whose weight must shift depending on whether the subject is a citizen or an institution wielding public or economic power.
