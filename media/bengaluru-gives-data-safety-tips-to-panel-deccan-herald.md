## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">

<p>A crucial consultation ahead of the framing of the country's data protection laws witnessed animated discussions here on Saturday.</p>

<p>Participants raised a variety of concerns. Held on the IISc campus, it discussed everything from revenge porn and human genomics to artificial intelligence and the right to be forgotten.</p>

<p>Cybersecurity experts, academics, lawyers and others attended the day-long event.</p>

<p>They made their submissions to the Srikrishna Committee, formed on July 31 last year to frame principles for data protection laws.</p>

<p>The session was chaired by Justice B N Srikrishna, retired Supreme Court judge. Also on the panel were Rama Vedashree, CEO, Data Security Council of India, and Gopalakrishnan S.</p>

<p>The basis of the discussion was a 200-page document drafted by the nine members of the Srikrishna Committee. January 31 is the deadline to respond to the committee's white paper.</p>

<p><strong>Classification of data</strong></p>

<p>Several dystopian scenarios, such as profiling and discrimination with the help of behavioural and psychometric data, led to discussions on the need for classification of data types.</p>

<p>Darshana, a lawyer from the People's Union of Civil Liberties (PUCL), spoke about how people were being denied rations for not holding Aadhaar.</p>

<p>The collection of children's biometric data brought up the question of consent.</p>

<p>Srikrishna clarified the white paper contained a chapter on consent: it suggests an age limit below which parental consent will have to be mandatory.</p>

<p>A discussion on the right to be forgotten arose after some participants sought a provision to revoke consent already given.</p>

<p>Questions associated with genome sequencing were raised by Vijay Chandru, professor, IISc.</p>

<p>"We need to pay special attention to this type of information. The collection of DNA in the form of saliva, when, say, you make a visit to a weight loss clinic, has become the commercial norm. The Insurance Regulatory Act can have huge implications as genetic data can be used to discriminate and deny health coverage," Chandru said.</p>

<p>Sunil Abraham, head of the Centre for Internet and Society, said he was delighted with the quality of debate and discussion.</p>

## Context and Background

The Srikrishna Committee was constituted in July 2017 to draft a data protection framework for India. Its mandate followed the Supreme Court's privacy judgment in *Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India*, and the committee held a series of public consultations to gather input from civil society, academia, industry and legal practitioners before the 31 January 2018 deadline for responses to its white paper.

The Bengaluru consultation at the Indian Institute of Science touched on a notably wide range of concerns, from biometric data and children's consent to genetic information and platform accountability. That breadth reflected how comprehensive the proposed law would need to be, and how far India had yet to travel in building a workable regulatory framework for personal data.

The session also brought together positions that did not always sit easily together. Industry interests, civil liberties advocates, and technical experts each brought different priorities to the table. The eventual Personal Data Protection Bill, introduced in Parliament in 2019, drew directly on the committee's recommendations from this consultation process.
