<p><strong>The Aadhaar card is not compulsory for citizens and "no person should be denied any benefits or 'suffer' for not having the Aadhaar cards issued by Unique Identification Authority of India," the Supreme Court ruled on Monday.</strong></p>

<p>Four years after Aadhaar was launched â€“ and touted as a panacea to access social services and subsidies â€“ its users continue to be dogged by an array of problems ranging from technical glitches to procedural delays. And those who do not have an Aadhaar card find themselves quizzed by government authorities.</p>

<p><em>The Hindu</em>'s Tamil Nadu edition today highlighted the challenges ordinary citizens - both those who have cards and those who do not â€“ face, be it from non-availability of application forms or glitches in the biometrics process.</p>

<p>We will be hosting a live chat on Aadhaar at 5 pm today. You can pose questions and share your views with Sunil Abraham, Executive Director of Bangalore-based research organisation, Centre for Internet and Society; K. Gopinath, Professor at the Computer Science and Automation Department at the Indian Institute of Science (IISc) and <em>The Hindu</em>'s K. Venkatraman.</p>

<p><strong>Comment From Anon</strong><br>

<p><strong>Sunil Abraham:</strong> Usually the party that is in power is pro-surveillance and anti-censorship and the opposition is pro-privacy and pro-free speech. After the elections - if the parties swap positions as a result of the mandate - then they usually also swap positions on surveillance and censorship. This phenomenon is not specific to India.</p>

<p><strong>K. Gopinath:</strong> The leakage in the current models is very high. Hence, the attraction.</p> 

<p>The issue earlier was whether there was some costs to the use of sw (esp. proprietary) from outside the country. Probably, these have been addressed.</p>

<p><strong>Comment From Saurabh</strong><br>
Aadhaar was supposed to be a good 2 factor authentication mechanism, what happens to it now?</p>

<p><strong>Sunil Abraham:</strong> Aadhaar architecture was designed to allow for multiple authentication factors. Unfortunately biometrics is a poor authentication factor since it cannot be revoked. Any two-factor authentication scheme where one factor is biometrics is in reality only a one-factor scheme. Pin code as with credit cards and debit cards would have been much more secure for authentication.</p>

<p><strong>K Venkataramanan:</strong> It will continue to be relevant, but is unlikely to be mandatory for quite some time.</p>

<p><strong>K. Gopinath:</strong> Real-time 2-factor auth (biometrics, signatures) are not easy, esp over Internet, and would require a much longer rollout</p>

<p><strong>The Hindu:</strong> Is Aadhaar virtually redundant now following the SC order? Nothing more than an expensive experiment?</p>

<p><strong>K. Gopinath:</strong> I think it will be used as an addl auth mechanism (just like elec./ph. receipts). May be once the technology is demo'ed properly (it has not been done seriously anywhere else), it will be taken up again.</p>

This only prevents access to service but other attacks can exfiltrate (take out) data, modify data, etc.</p>

<p><strong>Sunil Abraham:</strong> With or without laws. Centralized approaches to identity/authentication management are much more fragile and vulnerable compared to decentralized options. The Internet is secured by digital signatures - there is no centralized repository of all these signatures. Therefore there is no centralized point of failure for the Internet. If the Aadhaar project was based on Smart Cards instead of Biometrics - then just like the Internet it would be robust without a central point of failure. http://cis-india.org/intern...</p>

<p><strong>K. Gopinath:</strong> Storing all info in a single place is a big security risk. It needs very robust technologies (such as replication and "secret sharing protocols") that work inspite of failures. These have been done here and there but doing it on a large scale requires care.</p>

## Context and Background

The live chat took place against the backdrop of sustained judicial scrutiny of the Aadhaar scheme, which had been launched in 2009 without dedicated parliamentary legislation. The Supreme Court's order on 23 September 2013 had established that Aadhaar could not be mandatory, and subsequent orders in March 2015 reinforced that the scheme remained voluntary pending a final constitutional determination.

The Standing Committee on Finance had in December 2011 rejected the National Identification Authority of India Bill 2010, citing concerns about conceptual clarity, cost assessment, privacy safeguards and the extension of enrolment to non-citizens. The government had nevertheless continued the rollout of Aadhaar, prompting repeated interventions by the Court to clarify that no person could be denied services or subsidies for want of the card.

The discussion foregrounds tensions between technological capacity and legal frameworks, particularly around the architecture of biometric authentication, centralised data storage and the absence of robust data protection legislation. Participants raised recurring concerns about security vulnerabilities, authentication failures in field conditions, quality control in outsourced enrolment processes and the lack of clear policies governing access to and retention of data collected by UIDAI.
