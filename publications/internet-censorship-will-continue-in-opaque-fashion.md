## Full Text

<div class="highlighted-text" id="fulltext">
  <p>A division bench of the Supreme Court has ruled on three sections of the Information Technology Act 2000 ‚Äî Section 66A, Section 79 and Section 69A.</p>

  <p>The draconian Section 66A was originally meant to tackle spam and cyber-stalking but was used by the powerful elite to crack down on online dissent and criticism.</p>

  <p>Section 79 was meant to give immunity to internet intermediaries for liability emerging from third-party speech, but it had a chilling effect on free speech because intermediaries erred on the side of caution when it came to deciding whether the content was legal or illegal.</p>

  <p>And Section 69A was the web blocking or internet censorship provision, but the procedure prescribed did not adhere to the principles of natural justice and transparency. For instance, when books are banned by courts, the public is informed of such bans but when websites are banned in India, there‚Äôs no clear message from the Internet Service Provider.</p>

  <p>The Supreme Court upheld 69A, so web blocking and internet censorship in India will continue to happen in an opaque fashion which is worrying. But on 66A and 79, the landmark judgment protects the right to free speech and expression. It struck down 66A in entirety, saying the vague and imprecise language made the provision unconstitutional and it interfered with ‚Äúthe right of the people to know ‚Äì the marketplace of ideas ‚Äì which the internet provides to persons of all kinds‚Äù. However, it only read down Section 79 saying ‚Äúunlawful acts beyond what is laid down‚Äù as reasonable restrictions to the right to free speech in the Constitution ‚Äúobviously cannot form any part‚Äù of the section. In short, the court has eliminated any additional restrictions for speech online even though it admitted that the internet is ‚Äúintelligibly different‚Äù from traditional media and might require additional laws to be passed by the Indian Parliament.</p>

  <p>‚Äî <em>Sunil Abraham, Executive Director, The Centre for Internet and Society</em></p>

## Context and Background

This article was written in the immediate aftermath of the Supreme Court's Shreya Singhal v. Union of India verdict (March 2015), which struck down Section 66A of the IT Act and clarified intermediary liability under Section 79.  

While many celebrated the ruling as a victory for digital freedom, Sunil Abraham cautions that the judgment left Section 69A, India‚Äôs website blocking provision, fully intact ‚Äî allowing censorship to persist through opaque and unaccountable processes.
